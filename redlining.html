OUR INTERACTIVE MAP

The interactive map displayed above shows how historical redlining practices have shaped Austin’s racial and economic landscape. The interactive map above shows a “redlining” map drawn by the Home Owners' Loan Corporation (HOLC) in July 1934 layered on top of a map of Austin today. Established as part of the New Deal, the HOLC was designed to assist homeowners who were defaulted on mortgages. These redlining maps were drawn to classify neighborhoods by levels of perceived lending risk based on economic and racial composition.

This Austin map in particular contains 18 residential zones in Austin’s metropolitan area, ranking them from A - Best, to D - Hazardous. Note how many of the “D” ranked regions still have low median incomes and high minority populations.

HISTORICAL REDLINING IN AUSTIN

This practice of isolating minorities began with the implementation of a “Negro District” in 1928, which made East Austin the only place where African Americans could access schools and other public services. Many neighborhoods with the restriction that properties could only be sold to whites, most famously Hyde Park, were also built around this time.

These explicitly racist housing restrictions were eventually removed, but were replaced by more subtle means. Since African Americans possessed less generational wealth and opportunities for employment, they could not afford to move into these richer neighborhoods, and as a result continued to be denied access to Austin’s best schools, parks, etc.

EFFECTS OF REDLINING

Redlining continues to be a problem in Austin. This can be seen in how Austin is one of the most racially and economically segregated metro areas in the US. Austin continues to be racially divided along I-35: African American and Hispanic populations remain concentrated east of the highway, while the population west of the highway is predominantly white. This continued exclusion of minorities from good households and neighborhoods has led to crime and poverty being concentrated in East Austin, reinforcing the lack of private investment in these communities.
